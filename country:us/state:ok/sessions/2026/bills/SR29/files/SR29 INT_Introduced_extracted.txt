6
7 Black History Month; recounting legislative actions;
8 remembering the roles of Black Oklahomans in the
8 Civil Rights Movement; and honoring the contributions
9 of Black Oklahomans and recognizing 100 years of
9 Black History.
10
10
11
11
12 WHEREAS, 2026 marks the 100th year of celebrating Black History
12
13 in the United States; and
13
14 WHEREAS, the first bill passed in the Oklahoma State Senate
14
15 after Oklahoma’s admission as a state was Senate Bill 1, requiring
15
16 separate facilities for Black people in public transportation,
16
17 education, and other public facilities. One hundred years later,
17
18 the Legislature approved Senate Concurrent Resolution 49,
18
19 repudiating Oklahoma’s Jim Crow laws; and
19
20 WHEREAS, Black people sought opportunity in Oklahoma long before
20
21 statehood, establishing 50 all-Black towns throughout Oklahoma and
21
22 Indian Territory. Communities like Boley, Red Bird, and Langston
22
23 became prominent and thriving towns with banks, businesses, and
23
24 schools. Thirteen of those communities still exist today. Those
24
Req. No. 3676 Page 1
1 comm unities and other important locations were highlighted in the
1
2 creation of the Oklahoma Civil Rights Trail, the result of
2
3 bipartisan legislation approved in 2023; and
3
4 WHEREAS, the events of the Tulsa Race Massacre of 1921 are
4
5 believed to be the single worst incident of racial violence in
5
6 American history. Long ignored and hidden, those events were
6
7 brought to light when, in 1997, Representative Don Ross and Senator
7
8 Maxine Horner, both of Tulsa, championed legislation creating a
8
9 commission which ultimately released a 200-page report containing an
9
10 official history of the massacre. Today, the Greenwood Cultural
10
11 Center and Greenwood Rising continue to tell that story, honoring
11
12 the past and inspiring the future; and
12
13 WHEREAS, in 1897, the Oklahoma Territorial Legislature approved
13
14 House Bill 151, creating what is now known as Langston University,
14
15 providing Black students with an industrial and agricultural
15
16 curriculum, a teacher’s college, and a liberal arts curriculum.
16
17 Prominent students included Ada Lois Sipuel Fisher, whose later
17
18 efforts led to the desegregation of the University of Oklahoma
18
19 College of Law; Nancy Randolph Davis, inspired by Sipuel Fisher,
19
20 became the first Black student to attend what is now Oklahoma State
20
21 University; Maxine Horner, one of the first two Black women to serve
21
22 in the Oklahoma State Senate, the other being Vicki Miles-LaGrange;
22
23 Bessie Coleman, the first woman of color in the United States to
23
24 earn a pilot license; and many others; and
24
Req. No. 3676 Page 2
1 WHEREAS, cities throughout the state continue to draw attention
1
2 to the contributions of Oklahomans during the Civil Rights Movement,
2
3 including the recently dedicated Clara Luper National Sit-In Plaza
3
4 in downtown Oklahoma City, located on the original site of the Katz
4
5 Drug Store in Oklahoma City where Luper and members of the Oklahoma
5
6 City NAACP Youth Council staged a nonviolent protest in 1958 to help
6
7 bring an end to segregation. A Clara Luper Civil Rights Center is
7
8 also planned in Oklahoma City; and
8
9 WHEREAS, throughout the state’s history, Black Oklahomans have
9
10 made significant contributions in politics, business, medicine,
10
11 literature, music, and more, including Hannah Diggs Atkins, who was
11
12 the first Black woman to serve in the Oklahoma House of
12
13 Representatives and later served as Secretary of State under
13
14 Governor Henry Bellmon, among many other accomplishments. J.C.
14
15 Watts Jr., a former University of Oklahoma quarterback, served as
15
16 the state’s first Black United States Congressman. Prior to being
16
17 elected to Congress, Watts was elected to the Corporation
17
18 Commission, becoming the first Black Oklahoman to win statewide
18
19 office. Green Currin was the first Black Oklahoman to serve in the
19
20 Oklahoma Territorial Legislature in 1890, while A.C. Hamlin became
20
21 the first Black Oklahoman elected to the Oklahoma Legislature in
21
22 1908. E. Melvin Porter became the first Black man elected to the
22
23 Oklahoma State Senate in 1964. Ralph Waldo Ellison was an American
23
24 writer, critic, and scholar, best known for his novel Invisible Man,
24
Req. No. 3676 Page 3
1 and became the most widely known man of letters in America.
1
2 Historian, educator, and author John Hope Franklin, best known for
2
3 his seminal 1947 book From Slavery to Freedom: A History of African
3
4 Americans, was awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom, the
4
5 highest U.S. civilian honor. Charlie Christian won acclaim as an
5
6 American swing and jazz guitarist and was among the first electric
6
7 guitarists, transforming electric guitar from a rhythm instrument
7
8 into a leading solo instrument. Oklahoma’s Leona Mitchell sang for
8
9 18 seasons as a leading spinto soprano at the Metropolitan Opera in
9
10 New York and performed for five U.S. presidents. Actress and
10
11 producer Alfre Woodard has starred in film, television, and theater,
11
12 winning multiple awards, including four Emmys, a Golden Globe, and
12
13 three Screen Actors Guild Awards and was nominated for an Academy
13
14 Award, a BAFTA, and two Grammy Awards. These are but a few of the
14
15 many remarkable Black Oklahomans whose accomplishments have brought
15
16 positive attention to their professions and to Oklahoma.
16
17 NOW, THEREFORE, BE IT RESOLVED BY THE SENATE OF THE 2ND SESSION
17
18 OF THE 60TH OKLAHOMA LEGISLATURE:
18
19 THAT the Oklahoma State Senate recognizes and celebrates
19
20 February as Black History Month.
20
21 THAT the Oklahoma State Senate recognizes and applauds the
21
22 contribution of Black Oklahomans in the Civil Rights Movement and
22
23 efforts by communities and by the state to highlight those
23
24 accomplishments for future generations, and honors the
24
Req. No. 3676 Page 4
1 acco mplishments of Black Oklahomans who have been trailblazers in
1
2 their fields, earning state and national recognition for their
2
3 talent and dedication.
